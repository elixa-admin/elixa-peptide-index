How the Elixa Peptide Index Works

How we evaluate and index peptides — in plain English

⚠ This is not medical advice

Everything on Elixa is for research and educational purposes only. None of it should be taken as a recommendation to use, buy, or avoid any compound. Always speak to a doctor before making any health decisions.

How we rate each peptide

Every peptide gets an evidence rating based on three weighted questions:

1

Has it been tested in real people? — 50% of the score

This carries the most weight. If a compound has been through proper clinical trials — studies conducted in actual humans, published in medical journals — that's the most reliable evidence we have. Semaglutide has been studied in tens of thousands of people. BPC-157 has almost no human trial data at all. That difference matters enormously.

2

What do lab and animal studies show? — 25% of the score

Before human trials happen, compounds are tested in labs and animals. This evidence is promising, but it doesn't always translate to the same effects in humans. It's a meaningful signal — not a guarantee.

3

What are real-world users reporting? — 25% of the score

Community observations from people who have used these compounds provide useful real-world signal. We include this as context — not as scientific proof.

The Research Confidence bar

Each peptide detail page shows a single bar — from Experimental on the left to Clinically Validated on the right. This tells you, at a glance, how much trust to place in the evidence. A peptide sitting at "Clinically Validated" has robust human trial data and regulatory approval. One at "Experimental" is interesting but largely untested in humans.

The Landscape Map

The map shows all peptides plotted by two things: how strong the research is (left to right) and how widely approved it is by health regulators like the FDA and SAHPRA (bottom to top). Compounds in the top-right are approved and well-studied. Bottom-left means early research only. Bubble size reflects overall evidence strength.

South Africa context

Every entry includes a note specific to South African availability and legal status. SAHPRA is South Africa's medicines regulator — if a compound isn't SAHPRA-approved, it exists in a legal grey area regardless of its FDA or other international status.

The Evidence Score Formula
T1 = Human Studies Score  ×  0.50
T2 = Animal & Lab Score  ×  0.25
T3 = Community Signal Score  ×  0.25
────────────────────────────────────
Composite = (T1 × 0.50) + (T2 × 0.25) + (T3 × 0.25)
Each tier is scored 0–10. The composite produces a final score out of 10, which maps to the Research Confidence bar position.
